Introduction

This is a 3x2 matrix. Columns by rows; counterintuitive! $$\underbrace{\left[ \begin{array}{ccc}a & b \\c & d \\ e& f \end{array} \right]}_\textrm{2 columns}\textrm{3 rows}$$ Matrices are denoted by capital letters, like $A$.

Basic operations

Addition

This applies to any dimensions, and applies to subtraction, too. $$\left[ \begin{array}{cc}a & b \\c & d \end{array} \right] +\left[ \begin{array}{cc}e & f \\g & h \end{array} \right]= \left[ \begin{array}{cc}a+e & b+f \\c+g & d+h \end{array} \right]$$ However, for addition, both matrices must have the same dimensions. $$\left[ \begin{array}{cc}a & b \\c & d \end{array} \right] +\left[ \begin{array}{c}a & b \end{array} \right]=\textrm{undefined}$$

Multiplication

Multiplying a matrix by a scalar is very easy. $$x\left[ \begin{array}{cc}a & b \\c & d \end{array} \right] =\left[ \begin{array}{cc}xa & xb \\xc & xd \end{array} \right]$$ Multiplying a matrix by a matrix is a bit more complicated. Go right on each row while down on each column. $$\left[ \begin{array}{cc}\textcolor{red}{a} & \textcolor{blue}{b} \\c & d \end{array} \right] \left[ \begin{array}{cc}\textcolor{red}{e} & f \\\textcolor{blue}{g} & h \end{array} \right]= \left[ \begin{array}{cc}\textcolor{red}{ae}+\textcolor{blue}{bg} & af+bh \\ce+dg & cf+dh \end{array} \right] $$ $$\left[ \begin{array}{cc}a & b \\c & d \end{array} \right]\left[ \begin{array}{c} e\\f \end{array} \right]=\left[ \begin{array}{c}ae+bf \\ce+df \end{array} \right]$$ If matrix A has dimensions $a*b$ and matrix B has dimensions $c*d$, matrix AB is defined iff $b==c$ and has dimensions $a*d$.

Determinant

The determinant of $A$ is denoted as $\textrm{det}(A)$ or $|A|$
A matrix can have a determinant iff its dimensions are $n*n$ (It has the same number of rows and columns)

2x2

$$\textrm{det}\left[ \begin{array}{cc}a & b \\c & d \end{array} \right]=ad-bc$$

3x3

Lattice method

It is also called the diagonal method. Write out the first two columns next to the matrix. Multiply diagonals of 3 numbers from left to right, and get their sum. Then subtract the sum of diagonals of 3 numbers from right to left. $$\left| \begin{array}{ccc} \textcolor{green}{a} & \textcolor{blue}{b} & \textcolor{red}{c} \\ d & \textcolor{green}{e}&\textcolor{blue}{f} \\ g& h &\textcolor{green}{i} \end{array}\right| \begin{array}{cc}a & b \\ \textcolor{red}{d}&e\\ \textcolor{blue}{g}&\textcolor{red}{h}\end{array}- \left| \begin{array}{ccc} a & b & \textcolor{blueviolet}{c} \\ d & \textcolor{blueviolet}{e}&\textcolor{skyblue}{f} \\ \textcolor{blueviolet}{g}& \textcolor{skyblue}h &\textcolor{orange}{i} \end{array}\right| \begin{array}{cc}\textcolor{skyblue}a & \textcolor{orange}b \\ \textcolor{orange}d&e\\ g&h\end{array}$$ $$(\textcolor{green}{aei}+\textcolor{blue}{bfg}+\textcolor{red}{cdh})-(\textcolor{blueviolet}{ceg}+\textcolor{skyblue}{afh}+\textcolor{orange}{bdi})$$

Expansion by minors

To find the minor of element $a$ in a matrix, cross out the row and column and the matrix remaining is the minor. $$\left[ \begin{array}{ccc}\textcolor{red}{a} & \enclose{horizontalstrike}b & \enclose{horizontalstrike}c\\\enclose{horizontalstrike}d & e & f\\\enclose{horizontalstrike}g & h & i \end{array} \right]\rightarrow \left[ \begin{array}{cc}e&f\\h&i \end{array} \right]$$ Find the determinants of the minors of three elements in the same row/column, and multiply them each by the elements. Also multiply by the sign for each element in the matrix $\left[ \begin{array}{ccc}+&-&+\\-&+&-\\+&-&+ \end{array} \right]$.For example, the three elements chosen in matrix $A$ can be $a$, $b$, and $c$. $$|A|=a\left| \begin{array}{cc}e&f\\h&i \end{array} \right|-b\left| \begin{array}{cc}d&f\\g&i \end{array} \right|+c\left| \begin{array}{cc}d&e\\g&h \end{array} \right|$$

Inverse

A matrix $A$ has an inverse iff $\textrm{det}(A)\neq0$

2x2

$${\left[ \begin{array}{cc}a&b\\c&d \end{array} \right]}^{-1}=\frac{\left[ \begin{array}{cc}d&-b\\-c&a \end{array} \right]}{\left| \begin{array}{cc}a&b\\c&d \end{array} \right|}$$ $\left[ \begin{array}{cc}d&-b\\-c&a \end{array} \right]$ is a "flippy matrix"; flip $a$ and $d$ and negate $b$ and $d$.

3x3

$$\textrm{Let }A=\left[ \begin{array}{ccc}a&b&c\\d&e&f\\g&h&i \end{array} \right]$$ Find the cofactor matrix (matrix of minors); for each element, replace it with the determinant of its minor. Also multiply by the sign for each element in the matrix $\left[ \begin{array}{ccc}+&-&+\\-&+&-\\+&-&+ \end{array} \right]$. $$ \left[ \begin{array}{ccc} +\left| \begin{array}{cc}e&f\\h&i\end{array} \right| &-\left| \begin{array}{cc}d&f\\g&i\end{array} \right| &+\left| \begin{array}{cc}d&e\\g&h\end{array} \right|\\ -\left| \begin{array}{cc}b&c\\h&i\end{array} \right| &+\left| \begin{array}{cc}a&c\\g&i\end{array} \right| &-\left| \begin{array}{cc}a&b\\g&h\end{array} \right|\\ +\left| \begin{array}{cc}b&c\\e&f\end{array} \right| &-\left| \begin{array}{cc}a&c\\d&f\end{array} \right| &+\left| \begin{array}{cc}a&b\\d&e\end{array} \right|\\ \end{array} \right] $$ Let the cofactor matrix be $\left[ \begin{array}{ccc}q&r&s\\t&u&v\\w&x&y \end{array} \right]$. Now find the transpose of that matrix, which means to switch the rows and columns. $$\left[ \begin{array}{ccc}q&r&s\\t&u&v\\w&x&y \end{array} \right]\rightarrow\left[ \begin{array}{ccc}q&t&w\\r&u&x\\s&v&y \end{array} \right]$$ This is called the adjugate of matrix $A$. To find the inverse, divide the adjugate by the determinant of the original matrix. $$A^{-1}=\frac{\left[ \begin{array}{ccc}q&t&w\\r&u&x\\s&v&y \end{array} \right]}{|A|}$$

Identity

The identity matrix is the matrix equivalent of 1:
  • Multiplying matrix $A$ by the identity matrix gives you $A$.
  • Multiplying matrix $A$ by its inverse $A^{-1}$ gives you the identity matrix.
The 2x2 identity matrix is $\left[ \begin{array}{cc}1&0\\0&1 \end{array} \right]$. The 3x3 identity matrix is $\left[ \begin{array}{ccc}1&0&0\\0&1&0\\0&0&1 \end{array} \right]$.

Systems with Cramer's Rule

Cramer's rule is an efficient way of solving systems of 2 and 3+ variables.

2 variables

$$ax+by=e$$ $$cx+dy=f$$ $$A=\left[ \begin{array}{cc}a&b\\c&d\end{array} \right]$$ $$x=\frac{\left[ \begin{array}{cc}e&b\\f&d\end{array} \right]}{|A|}, y=\frac{\left[ \begin{array}{cc}a&e\\c&f\end{array} \right]}{|A|}$$ To solve for a variable, replace the column containing that variable's coefficients with the coefficients which are just numbers.

3+ variables

Very similar to 2 variables: $$ax+by+cz=j$$ $$dx+ey+fz=k$$ $$gx+hy+iz=l$$ $$A=\left[ \begin{array}{ccc}a&b&c\\d&e&f\\g&h&i\end{array} \right]$$ $$x=\frac{\left[ \begin{array}{ccc}j&k&l\\d&e&f\\g&h&i\end{array} \right]}{|A|}, y=\frac{\left[ \begin{array}{ccc}a&b&c\\j&k&l\\g&h&i\end{array} \right]}{|A|}, z=\frac{\left[ \begin{array}{ccc}a&b&c\\d&e&f\\j&k&l\end{array} \right]}{|A|}$$ Similarly, this can apply to systems of equations for 4+ variables, although most people use computers/calculators for those.

Systems with Inverses

$$ax+by=e$$ $$cx+dy=f$$ $$\left [ \begin{array}{c}x\\y\end{array} \right]=\left[ \begin{array}{cc}a&b\\c&d\end{array} \right]^{-1}\left [ \begin{array}{c}e\\f\end{array} \right]$$

Matrix Equations

$$\left[ \begin{array}{ccc}a&b&c\\d&e&f\\g&h&i \end{array} \right]X=\left[ \begin{array}{ccc}j&k&l\\m&n&o\\p&q&r \end{array} \right]$$ To normally solve for x, one would divide both sides by the matrix $\left[ \begin{array}{ccc}a&b&c\\d&e&f\\g&h&i \end{array} \right]$. However, to "divide" with matrices, you must multiply by the inverse instead. $$X=\left[ \begin{array}{ccc}a&b&c\\d&e&f\\g&h&i \end{array} \right]^{-1}\left[ \begin{array}{ccc}j&k&l\\m&n&o\\p&q&r \end{array} \right]$$ When you move the inverse of the matrix to the other side, always move it to the left of the existing matrix. (Presumably, if it's $XA=B$, you would move it to the right instead?)

Area of Triangle

The area of a triangle with points $(x_{1},y_{1})$, $(x_{2},y_{2})$, and $(x_{3},y_{3})$ is: $$\pm\frac{1}{2}\left| \begin{array}{ccc}x_{1}&y_{1}&1\\x_{2}&y_{2}&1\\x_{3}&y_{3}&1 \end{array} \right|$$ $\pm$ is used in place of an absolute value sign to avoid confusion with the determinant; area can only be positive.
