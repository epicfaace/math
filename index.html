Calculus

Integrals

Indefinite

Indefinite integral is the antiderivative:
$$\int f'(x)\,dx=f(x)+C$$
C is a constant, since $\frac{d}{dx}f(x)=\frac{d}{dx}(f(x)+C)$

Properties:

Assuming a and b are constants: $$\int f(x)+g(x)\,dx=\int f(x)\,dx+\int g(x)\,dx$$ $$\int ax^b\,dx=\frac{a}{b+1}a^{b+1}$$ $$\int b\,dx=\int b^{0}\,dx=bx$$

Matrices

This is a 3x2 matrix. Columns by rows; counterintuitive! $$\underbrace{\left[ \begin{array}{ccc}a & b \\c & d \\ e& f \end{array} \right]}_\textrm{2 columns}\textrm{3 rows}$$ Matrices are denoted by capital letters, like $A$.

Basic operations

Addition

This applies to any dimensions, and applies to subtraction, too. $$\left[ \begin{array}{cc}a & b \\c & d \end{array} \right] +\left[ \begin{array}{cc}e & f \\g & h \end{array} \right]= \left[ \begin{array}{cc}a+e & b+f \\c+g & d+h \end{array} \right]$$ However, for addition, both matrices must have the same dimensions. $$\left[ \begin{array}{cc}a & b \\c & d \end{array} \right] +\left[ \begin{array}{c}a & b \end{array} \right]=\textrm{undefined}$$

Multiplication

Multiplying a matrix by a scalar is very easy. $$x\left[ \begin{array}{cc}a & b \\c & d \end{array} \right] =\left[ \begin{array}{cc}xa & xb \\xc & xd \end{array} \right]$$ Multiplying a matrix by a matrix is a bit more complicated. $$\left[ \begin{array}{cc}a & b \\c & d \end{array} \right] \left[ \begin{array}{cc}e & f \\g & h \end{array} \right]= \left[ \begin{array}{cc}ae+bg & af+bh \\ce+dg & cf+dh \end{array} \right] $$ If matrix A has dimensions $a*b$ and matrix B has dimensions $c*d$, matrix AB is defined iff $b==c$ and has dimensions $a*d$.

Determinant

The determinant of $A$ is denoted as $\textrm{det}(A)$ or $|A|$
A matrix can have a determinant iff its dimensions are $n*n$ (It has the same number of rows and columns)
